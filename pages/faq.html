Government Jobs

How do I know if a government job listing is real?

Every government job listing on TrabahoWatch is sourced from official portals: the CSC Job Portal, DepEd regional websites, DOH official site, LGU portals, and other official agency announcements.

Each listing includes a "Source" link to the original official announcement. Always verify directly with the hiring agency's Human Resource Management Office (HRMO) before submitting documents.

Do I need CSC eligibility to apply for all government jobs?

For permanent (plantilla) positions: Yes — CSC eligibility is mandatory. Sub-Professional eligibility for SG 1–9 positions, Professional eligibility for SG 10+, or special eligibilities like RA 1080 (for licensed professionals).

For Casual, Job Order (JO), and Contractual positions: No CSC eligibility is required. These are short-term or project-based positions — useful for gaining government work experience while preparing for permanent positions.

For PNP and BFP uniformed positions: A separate entrance examination (not the CSC PPT) is required.

What is the Personal Data Sheet (PDS) and where do I get it?

The Personal Data Sheet (PDS), also known as CSC Form 212, is the official government application form required for all civil service positions. It is a detailed personal history form covering education, work experience, family, character references, and declarations.

Download the latest version from csc.gov.ph. Fill it out completely and honestly — false declarations are grounds for dismissal and criminal charges even years after appointment.

Can I apply to multiple government positions at the same time?

Yes. There is no restriction on applying to multiple government positions simultaneously, as long as each application is submitted independently to the respective agency's HRMPSB before the deadline.

Keep track of each application's deadline, required documents, and interview schedules. Prepare complete sets of documents for each application — sharing one set between applications is not recommended as processes may overlap.

What happens to my application after I submit it?

After submission, the agency's HRMPSB (Human Resource Merit Promotion and Selection Board) reviews all applications for completeness and eligibility. Shortlisted applicants are notified for the next stage — typically a demo teaching (for DepEd), a written exam (for NBI/LGU), or a panel interview.

The HRMPSB ranks candidates using the Comparative Assessment of Ranking (CAR) system, which weighs education, experience, training, performance, and interview results. The top-ranking candidate is recommended to the appointing authority (Secretary, Mayor, Governor, etc.) for appointment.

Follow up with the agency's HRMO 2 weeks after submission if you have not received any communication.

Salary Grades & Compensation

What is the salary of a Teacher I in 2026?

Under SSL VI Tranche 3 (effective January 1, 2026), a DepEd Teacher I at Salary Grade 11, Step 1 earns a basic monthly salary of ₱31,705.

Add ₱2,000 PERA monthly allowance for a gross monthly pay of ₱33,705. After GSIS (9%), PhilHealth (2.5%), and Pag-IBIG (₱100) deductions, estimated take-home pay is approximately ₱30,059/month.

Annual total compensation (including 13th month, mid-year bonus, year-end bonus, clothing allowance, cash gift, and PEI) is approximately ₱470,000/year.

Use the Salary Grade Calculator for personalized estimates.

What is PERA and is it included in the basic salary?

PERA stands for Personnel Economic Relief Allowance. It is a ₱2,000 monthly allowance given to all permanent government employees on top of their basic salary.

PERA is not part of the basic salary — it is a separate allowance. It is exempt from GSIS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG deductions, and is not counted in computing retirement benefits. It is purely additional take-home pay.

How does the step increment system work?

Government employees advance one step within their salary grade every 3 years of satisfactory or better performance. Each step is approximately 3% higher than the previous step. The maximum is Step 8.

For example: a Teacher I hired at SG 11 Step 1 (₱31,705) becomes Step 2 (≈₱32,656) after 3 years, Step 3 after 6 years, and so on — until Step 8, after which salary increases only come through promotion to a higher salary grade.

Step increments are independent of SSL tranche increases — both can apply in the same year.

When is the next SSL salary increase (Tranche 4)?

The fourth and final tranche of SSL VI (Executive Order No. 64, s. 2024) is scheduled to take effect on January 1, 2027. Tranche 3 (currently in effect) started January 1, 2026.

TrabahoWatch will update all salary figures and the calculator as soon as the official DBM salary schedule for Tranche 4 is released. Monitor dbm.gov.ph for the official announcement.

What are the government employee annual bonuses?

Permanent government employees receive these annual bonuses:

  • Mid-Year Bonus — 1 month basic salary, released in May
  • Year-End Bonus — 1 month basic salary, released in November
  • 13th Month Pay — 1 month basic salary, released in December
  • Cash Gift — ₱5,000, released with Year-End Bonus in November
  • PEI (Productivity Enhancement Incentive) — ₱5,000, released in December
  • Clothing Allowance — ₱6,000/year, released in the 1st semester

These bonuses add approximately 3–4 months' worth of basic salary to annual total compensation.

CSC Exam

What is the passing score for the CSC exam?

The passing score for both the Professional and Sub-Professional CSC Pen and Paper Test (PPT) is 80.00%. Scores below 80% are considered failed regardless of the applicant's qualifications or experience.

Once you pass, your eligibility is permanent — you never have to retake the exam. Your rating (whether 80% or 99%) is recorded on your Certificate of Eligibility and factors into your ranking for job applications.

How many times can I take the CSC exam?

You may take the CSC exam twice a year (once per examination period — March and August). There is no limit on the total number of attempts across multiple years.

However, you must wait at least 3 months from your previous attempt before retaking the same level (Professional or Sub-Professional).

What is the difference between Professional and Sub-Professional?

The Sub-Professional exam is the 1st Level eligibility — for positions SG 1–9 (clerical, support, aide positions). It is open to high school graduates or those with at least 2 years of college.

The Professional exam is the 2nd Level eligibility — for positions SG 10 and above (officers, specialists, professionals). It requires a bachelor's degree or enrollment in the final year of college.

Both exams have a passing score of 80.00% and a fee of ₱500. The Professional exam has 170 items, Sub-Professional has 165 items.

When is the next CSC exam?

The next CSC Pen and Paper Test (PPT) is scheduled for August 16, 2026 (Professional and Sub-Professional levels). Registration typically runs in June 2026 — monitor csc.gov.ph for the official schedule.

The following exam is tentatively scheduled for March 2027. TrabahoWatch tracks upcoming CSC exam dates in the CSC Exam Guide .

OFW Opportunities

How do I verify if an OFW recruitment agency is legitimate?

Verify any recruitment agency's license at dmwph.com before signing any contract or paying any fee. Only DMW/POEA-accredited agencies can legally deploy OFWs.

Report suspicious agencies to the DMW hotline: 1348 or email e-consular@dmwph.gov.ph . Illegal recruitment is a criminal offense in the Philippines.

What is the maximum placement fee an OFW recruiter can charge?

Under Philippine law, the maximum placement fee a DMW-accredited agency may charge is equivalent to one month's salary for the position you are being deployed for.

Agencies charging more than one month's salary are in violation of the law. Domestic helpers and household service workers typically cannot be charged any placement fee at all (this depends on the destination country's bilateral agreements with the Philippines).

Never pay any fee before a verified, DMW-stamped employment contract is in your hands.

What is PDOS and is it required?

PDOS stands for Pre-Departure Orientation Seminar. It is mandatory and free for all first-time OFWs. It is conducted by the DMW and covers your rights as an OFW, destination country laws and culture, emergency contacts, remittance options, and the OFW Record (e-Registration) process.

You cannot secure your OEC (Overseas Employment Certificate) without completing the PDOS. Online PDOS is available at pdos.dmwph.gov.ph.

What is an OEC and why does it matter at the airport?

The Overseas Employment Certificate (OEC) is the official government document proving you are a documented OFW deploying through legal channels. Present it at airport check-in and immigration to be exempt from:

  • Travel Tax (₱1,620)
  • Terminal Fee (₱550)
  • Immigration duty (no need for "balikbayan" stamp issues)

Process your OEC at any DMW office or through the BaM App (Bagong OFW App) before departure. Without it, you may be stopped at the airport.

Which OFW destinations offer permanent residency pathways?

The most established PR pathways for Filipino OFWs include:

  • Canada — Express Entry (Federal Skilled Worker), Provincial Nominee Programs (PNP). Nurses and other healthcare workers are priority categories.
  • Japan — After 3+ years as a Certified Care Worker (Kaigo Fukushishi) and passing the national exam, long-term residency and eventually permanent residency become available.
  • Australia — Skilled Migration visa (subclass 189/190) for qualified professionals.
  • Germany — EU Blue Card for skilled workers, with PR available after 33 months of employment.

Gulf countries (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait) are generally employment-only destinations with no PR pathway.

Private Sector Jobs

Are the private sector salaries on TrabahoWatch accurate?

Private sector salaries are inherently variable — they depend on company size, location, candidate experience, and negotiation. The salary ranges on TrabahoWatch are market estimates based on publicly available data from JobStreet, LinkedIn salary surveys, and industry reports for 2026.

We always show a range (e.g. ₱20,000–₱28,000) rather than a fixed amount for private sector positions. Always confirm actual compensation during the hiring process.

Do BPO companies really hire without experience?

Yes — entry-level BPO (call center) positions genuinely require no prior experience. The hiring requirement is good English communication skills, computer proficiency, and willingness to work shifting schedules including graveyard shifts.

BPO companies conduct their own training programs (typically 3–6 weeks paid training) before agents are deployed to live accounts. Fresh graduates and even senior high school graduates regularly get hired.

What benefits should I expect from a private sector job?

Mandatory benefits required by Philippine law for all private sector employees:

  • SSS (Social Security System) contributions
  • PhilHealth contributions
  • Pag-IBIG (HDMF) contributions
  • 13th Month Pay
  • 5 days Service Incentive Leave (SIL)

Large companies (especially BPO, banks, and hospitals) typically add: HMO coverage (often including dependents), annual performance bonuses, additional leave credits, and other non-mandatory benefits. Always confirm these during the job offer stage.
